What is a Career in Software

Software development is for people who have a passion for solving problems. You must enjoy constantly learning and improving your skills on your own. Most software is developed by a team so you must have the ability to work with others and be modest.

Solving problems IS WHAT WE DO! The tech industry changes rapidly. A career in software means self motivating to constantly stay up to date. Tech you know coming out of school will, best case, be uninteresting in 5 years. Worst case, irrelevant.

What is a Career in Software

There are many different roles one can have as part of a career in software.

  • Developer
  • Quality Assurance (Testing)
  • Systems Engineering
  • Release Engineer
  • DBA

Talk about variations on these roles. Qa - automation vs manual, developer - backend vs front end
SE == system admin. Strong networking, OS, automation background.

Education

In Software what you've done speaks louder than where you went to school or, even, what you studied.

A 2 or 4 year degree or IT training during a military career is generally required by employers. Computer Science is the traditional path for developers. It will give a great understanding of how computers work from top to bottom and will make you a better developer. Also, most likely, a higher salary.

A Computer Scientist and a Software Engineer are not exactly the same things. Some universities now offer Software Engineering focused programs or certificates.

Many people in software did not start with an engineering degree. CS degree commonly not required for QA, IT, SE type positions. But some other higher education is usually looked for. You learn much more than a specific topic in college. Employers want to see that you are capable of self guided learning and operating in an environment that isn't public school.

What Can You Do?

Just about anything! Every industry relies on software in some form or another. Your code could run on planes, cars, refrigerators, satelites or across thousands of machines in a data center.

Don't get stuck on picking a particular industry to work in. Focus on the technical problems that you'll be solving.

Question - Who knows what "The Cloud" actually is?

During a job hunt know any industries you won't work in (military, pharma, etc). If you do want to target a specific field (gaming, google, etc) be prepared to put in extra work to differentiate yourself from the pack. Learn industry specific tooling, methodologies.

Where can you Work?

Work environment and company culture can vary widely!

  • Solo
  • Remote
  • Small company (<100 people)
  • Large company (1000+ people)
  • Consulting - travel intensive or not

Industries vary widely! When searching for a job note if a company is a true software company or a company that has a software division. A software company's only product is software vs a company which makes something else and has a software group to support the other products.

Working remote has it's down sides.
  • Requires discipline
  • Miss out on lots of communication (i.e. if only part of team is remote)
  • Can make it harder to advance within a company
When working remote you can miss out on a lot of side conversations, whiteboard sessions and general face to face interaction with your team.

Where can you Work?

Finding a company that fits you culturally is just as important as finding one that will challenge you technically. This isn't just the one with the best free snacks!

  • How is this company going to help me progress technically?
  • Do they encourage the use of new technology?
  • Am I just going to be on-call 24/7?
  • Is leadership transparent about plans and goals?
  • Work/life balance?

A good software company should always be trying to make you a better software developer. Who wants to end up using the same tech for the next 20 years, or using a tech from 20 years ago? On-call rotations are not bad, and especially valuable starting out in your career Work/life balance is important but software is also fast paced field. Crunch time early in your career is expected and a good thing. Working hard because that's what needs to be done is one thing, killing yourself because of poor planning or leadership is another.

What would your schedule look like

Companies organize and prioritize work in different ways: Scrum, Agile, Kanban, Waterfall.

Agile example:

  • Teams generally 4-8 people.
  • Tasks divided into "stories" which typically take at most 2 weeks to complete.
  • Teams decide which stories they can complete in a 2 week cycle.
  • Daily stand-up to talk about work in progress.
  • At end of 2 weeks teams present work to stake holders. Process starts over.

Talk about contrast with waterfall model.

Market Yourself

You probably suck at writing a resume. And that's OK!

A resume filled with classes taken, previous job titles and vague descriptions of job functions does not tell employers much about who you are or what problems you enjoy solving. It also does not help you stand out among a pack of applicants.

Blogging, Tweeting and committing on GitHub are great ways to market yourself. This will help build your name in the industry and give you a place to point employers to see what kind of developer you are.

Marketing yourself is something I wish I had started much earlier on. Stuff to blog about: current project, how you go around/solved a certain problem, write tutorials, announce releases of projects you are working on, etc.
